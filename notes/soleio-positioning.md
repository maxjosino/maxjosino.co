# Soleio Positioning Notes

This file captures the positioning direction, public-signal strategy, and early draft writing we developed using the `soleio-design-hiring` skill in designer mode.

## Core Direction

- Current goal: build stronger public signal and inbound readiness without looking actively job-seeking
- Primary positioning: founding product designer with a hybrid profile across product design, systems, design engineering, and product engineering
- Strongest signal: builder mindset, `whatever it takes`, able to reduce ambiguity and cross boundaries when the product needs it
- Context that matters: founding product designer and first employee at Ground, operating in a pre-product-market-fit environment
- Key message: some of the highest-leverage design work in early-stage companies does not look like "design" in the traditional sense

## What We Are Optimizing For

- More precise public signal
- More discoverability for the right people
- Less generic "designer" framing
- No high-pressure job-search posture
- Low-volume, high-signal writing

## Audience / Company Archetype

The direction is most aligned with product-led, craft-conscious, engineering-adjacent companies where a hybrid designer-builder profile is unusually valuable.

Examples discussed:

- Cursor
- Vercel
- Replit
- Resend
- Lovable

Secondary lane:

- Nubank
- Brex
- Ramp

## Writing Principles

- Do not write like a content creator
- Do not over-explain
- Write from lived operating experience, not abstract advice
- Favor point of view over performance
- Keep posts short, high-signal, and grounded
- Focus on early-stage reality: ambiguity, structure, leverage, momentum

## Themes We Chose

1. `Whatever it takes`
2. `0->1 is ambiguity reduction`
3. `Founding design is an operating role`

These three are intended as a coherent sequence rather than isolated takes.

## Post Drafts v1 Final

### Post 01

```text
As a founding product designer and first employee, I’ve learned that some of the highest-leverage work I’ve done has barely looked like design.

At least not in the way people usually mean it.

Early-stage startups make one thing clear: there’s no script, no clean boundaries, and often not enough infrastructure.

Sometimes the highest-leverage design contribution is not another interface.

It’s doing whatever it takes to make the product work.
```

### Post 02

```text
A lot of 0→1 work is really ambiguity reduction.

Not just designing interfaces, but helping the team get clearer on what matters and turning product intent into something shippable.

That kind of clarity is often higher leverage than polish.
```

### Post 03

```text
Founding design work is often less about owning a surface and more about helping the product gain shape.

You’re not only designing what ships.
You’re helping create the conditions for the team to move, decide, and build with more clarity.

It’s an operating role as much as a design role.
```

## Ground Theme Bank

This section turns 4 years at Ground into reusable content themes. These are not full posts yet. They are thesis seeds that can become LinkedIn posts, site notes, or longer essays later.

### 1. Pre-PMF strips roles down to reality

Thesis:
Pre-product-market-fit exposes how artificial role boundaries can be. The work becomes whatever is most necessary to keep the product moving.

Why it matters:
This is one of the clearest foundations of the `whatever it takes` signal.

Possible angle:
"Pre-PMF doesn’t care what your title says."

### 2. Founding design is ambiguity reduction

Thesis:
Much of the real 0->1 job is not polished design output. It is helping the team understand what matters, what can wait, and what should be built next.

Why it matters:
This is central to how you describe leverage in early-stage work.

Possible angle:
"A lot of early product work is really about reducing ambiguity fast enough for the team to move."

### 3. The role expands before the title does

Thesis:
In early-stage companies, the scope of the work expands long before the title catches up. You end up operating across design, setup, support, systems, and execution.

Why it matters:
This explains the real lived shape of your Ground experience.

Possible angle:
"In startups, your role often expands before your title does."

### 4. Product structure is often a design contribution

Thesis:
One of the most valuable contributions a founding designer can make is creating structure: patterns, clarity, defaults, workflows, and ways for the team to make better product decisions.

Why it matters:
This is a strong bridge between design, systems, and product thinking.

Possible angle:
"Sometimes the design contribution is not the interface. It’s the structure behind it."

### 5. Systems are not a library problem

Thesis:
Systems only become valuable when they help a product team move with more clarity, consistency, and leverage. Otherwise they remain decorative artifacts.

Why it matters:
This can later become the bridge into a stronger design systems point of view.

Possible angle:
"A design system starts becoming useful when it stops being just a library."

### 6. Internal tools can be product work

Thesis:
Some of the highest-leverage work happens behind the scenes: internal tools, automations, and operational systems that improve visibility, reliability, and product execution.

Why it matters:
This is one of your clearest builder signals.

Possible angle:
"Not all meaningful product work is customer-facing."

### 7. The operational layer matters more than people think

Thesis:
Products rely on hidden operational layers: setup, support, data visibility, internal tooling, and system health. Neglecting these creates drag everywhere else.

Why it matters:
This is a more mature and less common point of view, especially for a design-led narrative.

Possible angle:
"A lot of product quality is decided in layers users never see."

### 8. First employee means absorbing entropy

Thesis:
Being the first non-founder or first design hire often means absorbing uncertainty and entropy so the rest of the company can move faster.

Why it matters:
This is a strong operating insight and gives language to invisible work.

Possible angle:
"A lot of early leverage comes from absorbing entropy."

### 9. Early-stage craft is not the same as polish

Thesis:
In early-stage work, craft is not just visual refinement. It is making decisions with taste under constraint and building enough structure for the product to survive and evolve.

Why it matters:
This lets you talk about craft without sounding aesthetic-only.

Possible angle:
"In early-stage, craft is not the same thing as polish."

### 10. Building trust often happens off-screen

Thesis:
Trust in a product is shaped not only by interfaces, but by whether setups work, support loops close, data is reliable, and internal systems hold up under pressure.

Why it matters:
This can become a strong, differentiated note about product quality and invisible design work.

Possible angle:
"A lot of user trust is earned in places users never directly see."
